---

For six centuries, the English Exchequer ran on tally sticks. A piece of willow, notched to record a debt or payment, then split lengthwise into two halves: the *stock* kept by the creditor, the *foil* kept by the debtor. The grain of the wood ran through both halves; only the original pair could ever be matched again, notch to notch and grain to grain. There was no central ledger, no possibility of reissuance, no one who could default on it — the wood itself was the record. Tallies circulated as bearer instruments. For long stretches of medieval and early-modern England, they were as close to money as the realm had.

The system was abolished by Act of Parliament in 1782, took effect in 1826, and by 1834 there were two cart-loads of accumulated tallies sitting in the Exchequer's stores, awaiting disposal. The Clerk of Works decided against giving them to staff as firewood. He chose instead to burn them in the under-floor stoves of the House of Lords. The stoves were rated for coal; the willow burned hotter and brighter than they could handle; the heat melted the copper flue lining; the floor caught fire. By morning, most of the medieval Palace of Westminster had been reduced to ash.

Charles Dickens, then a parliamentary reporter, observed afterward that the tallies could simply have been given away as firewood for the poor. But "official routine could not endure that they ever should be useful," and so the order went forth that they were to be privately burned. The over-stoked furnace set fire to the panelling, then to the Lords, then to the Commons. The two houses were reduced to ashes.

The fire is, in the strict reading, an accident of negligence. In a longer reading, it's the moment a 600-year-old decentralized record-keeping system was incinerated as accounting moved permanently to the central books of the Bank of England — completing a shift in which every citizen's money now depended on one institution not defaulting. The building that had housed the old way burned down on the way out the door.

---

**Every transaction layer trusts its infrastructure not to burn.** Tally sticks trusted the willow to survive — that the warehouse wouldn't catch fire, that water wouldn't rot it, that a clerk wouldn't decide it was kindling. Bank of England paper trusts the Bank to remain solvent, the state to remain solvent, the records to remain authoritative. Sovereign bonds trust the issuer. Equities trust the company. Stablecoins trust the custodian. Even other crypto-assets trust their foundations to keep funding development. Every form of money is a bet on someone else not defaulting — except one.

Bitcoin's only structural failure mode is that the internet stops existing. There is no issuer to default, no foundation to dissolve, no clerk to throw the records into the wrong stove. The ledger is reproduced in tens of thousands of independent locations; no single fire takes it. The bet, like the tally stick's, is structural to the medium itself — but the medium has gone from a single warehouse of willow to the entire global telecommunications fabric.

The world has so far chosen to use Bitcoin as a leveraged trade against the dollar — as global markets do with everything else: buy when its dollar price is low, sell when it's high, measure performance in dollars or euros. That use treats the most defensible transaction layer ever constructed as a speculative asset, alongside equities and bonds whose issuers absolutely will eventually default. Bitcoin isn't an asset to trade — it's the medium to use.

TallyKeep is built on the assumption that this gets noticed eventually. When it does, the people who understood early will need tools — not to *trade* Bitcoin, but to *use* it. To hold it as savings. To spend it as the current account. To send and receive without converting. Banking, in other words, on the tally that doesn't burn.

The mark you've seen on every page is a tally stick: the *stock* retained by you, the *foil* given to the other side, the grain of the wood the cryptographic hash that no one can forge a second time. The medium has changed. The structure is the same.
